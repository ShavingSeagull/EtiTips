Italy has a decent public transport network that has improved its reputation in recent years. The local trains, once chastised for their poor time keeping (how typically Italian!), are now held in higher regard following a splurge in infrastructure investment. The national high-speed trains are an ideal way to get from city to city and prove not to be overly expensive. Large cities, such as Rome, Milan, Turin and Naples also boast a metro system, which can be useful in avoiding the horrendous traffic!

Cars are driven on the right-hand side of the road.

Tipping

Taxis

- No tipping expected

As with much of the rest of Italy, tipping taxi drivers is by no means an expected custom. It is, however, rather common to see people rounding up the bill to the next Euro, but this is for matters of convenience in avoiding pockets of loose change rather than to provide a kind gesture to the driver. If you feel the need to give something, a Euro or two will definitely suffice. If you have booked a taxi on a pre-paid schedule (such as for picking you up from the airport and taking you to the hotel), you should give nothing extra, unless the driver helps you with your luggage.

Behaviour

Like the rest of the country, people are generally well behaved on public transport in Italy. Taking phone calls is permitted on transport, but it is best to keep your voice down as much as you can; if you find people glaring at you, you have probably been too loud. Contrary to the stereotype, most Italians aren't overly loud and won't appreciate it if you are, either.

Be sure to offer your seat to vulnerable people, such as the elderly or disabled, especially if they are women (and you are a man). Trains and the metro can get crowded during peak hours, so seating will be at a premium. Do the right thing and give a helping hand to those in need. You are representing your country when you go abroad, so making the effort to act courteously will leave a positive impression on the locals.

While it is fairly accepted to take your shoes off on the trains, don't under any circumstance put your feet (shoed or not) on the seat opposite. This is seen as dirty and extremely rude.

Do's

  • Speak with your indoor voice on public transport
  • Move inside the vehicle as soon as you board. It's best not to clog the door area
  • Give up your seat for somebody vulnerable

Don'ts

  • Raise your voice on public transport. Especially if you're doing so in English
  • Tip your taxi driver unless he has helped with luggage
  • Put your feet on the seat opposite
