Tunisia has a decent public transport system, focused largely on buses, trains and communal taxis. The rail system proves fairly reliable, though the service is sometimes prone to delays. Trains have first and second class carriages and if you're planning to travel long distance, we definitely recommend spending the extra for first class, as it is only slightly more expensive than second class, but the carriage is air conditioned, making a long journey much more enjoyable.

Cars are driven on the right-hand side of the road.

Tipping

Taxis

- Generally acceptable rate: Round up the bill

Tipping taxi drivers doesn't need to be hard work. Generally, it is safe to just round up the bill, though naturally this is reserved for good service and whether or not he has tried to rip you off. Regional taxis are yellow; longer distance ones are white. They all have meters and, contrary to what a lot of drivers will try and tell you, the meter works, too. Don't get into a taxi that tries to set a fixed price with you before departing, as this will be higher and aimed at tourists who don't know any better. Always insist on using the meter and if they refuse, find another taxi. Ask a local for an idea of the journey price before getting in a taxi.

Tunisia has a few differing taxi services. One of those, which is unlike any other service, is the Louage. This is a people carrier that holds 6-9 people and operates along the most popular routes. What makes them unique is that they take a mix of passengers, but won't leave until all the seats are full. If you are in a hurry - or just desire more space around you - you can pay for multiple seats. Sometimes, if there are other passengers who are just waiting for the final one, they will agree to split the cost of the final seat among them. Louages are a very popular, inexpensive and great way to get around in Tunisia. Unlike Morocco's Grand Taxis, which are involved in road accidents due to overworked drivers driving all day and night etc, the Tunisian Louages don't tend to operate after 7pm, so take this into account when planning your journey.

Bonus non-etiquette tip! Louages are colour-coded depending on their purpose. A red stripe on the side signifies long distance, a blue stripe is regional and a yellow stripe signifies it's local/rural. The place to pick one up is at a bus station in most towns. You can check a price list (as they operate on fixed routes despite being taxis) or ask a local. Bear in mind that the drivers usually only speak Arabic and French. You may find some broken English in tourist areas, but plan your route and gather the necessary information beforehand. Asking locals works well, as Louages are a popular method of travel here and priced similarly to the buses.

Behaviour

While Tunisians are generally friendly, don't expect much courtesy when using public transport. Buying tickets and embarking/disembarking can prove to be a bit of a free-for-all at times, so don't be afraid to stand up for yourself and get your place on the vehicle! Also, with people in close proximity on transport, be aware of pickpocketers. Tourists are always prime targets, so keep your belongings close to you and hidden, preferably under clothing. Keep any handbags/rucksacks in front of you and in your line of sight at all times.

Be sure to offer your seat to vulnerable people, such as the elderly or disabled, as seating will be at a premium, especially during busy hours. Do the right thing and give a helping hand to those in need. You are representing your country when you go abroad, so making the effort to act courteously will leave a positive impression on the locals, especially in a society that reveres its elders so much.

Do's

  • Make use of the Louages, which are a great way to get around and provide a good way of meeting local people!
  • Pay a little extra for first class on trains if travelling long distance
  • Give up your seat for somebody vulnerable

Don'ts

  • Get in a taxi whose driver claims the meter is broken. It isn't and he is trying to rip you off
  • Be afraid to ask a local about taxi route prices
  • Rent a car while in Tunisia. The drivers here tend to be...well...maniacs
