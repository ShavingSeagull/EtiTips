While many may not realise, Portugal is actually one of the oldest nation states in the world, with its status as an independent country being recognised for 900 years, but with a history dating well over 1000. Once a profound global power with an empire to match, Portugal is now a nation steeped in rich history, great food, traditional values and, of course, the home of one of the most famous football players of all-time: Cristiano Ronaldo.

The capital city is Lisbon and the currency is the Euro (€).

Behaviour

Greetings are an important social custom in Portugal. Whether meeting someone for the first time or the hundreth, you are expected to greet them when you meet and give a farewell when you part ways. Typically, this will be by using Bom dia/Boa tarde/Boa noite (good morning/ good afternoon/good evening), with a simple Adeus (goodbye) as a farewell. Note, too, that when greeting someone, a handshake or an air kiss is expected - a handshake for men (or a hug for friends) and an air kiss on both cheeks (starting with the right) for a man and a woman or two women. Yes, gents, even if you don't know the woman well, you are expected to greet with a kiss.

The Portuguese are a traditional, conservative bunch. To do well here, it is important to hold oneself well and act like people are watching. While the Portuguese can seem a little on the loud side when they speak, this is rarely a display of annoyance or anger and more just a part of the typical Latin passion that is common for the region. What does differ from their other Latin cousins, however, is the lack of gesticulation. Speaking with the hands is seen as boistrous and rude, so it is best to keep your wild limbs under control if you are one of those who lets their hands do the talking.

Unlike neighbouring Spain, the concept of personal space does exist in Portugal and about an arm's length is fine. It is, however, commonplace and an accepted practice to include physicality during conversation - think pats on the shoulder or touches of the arm etc. For some, this is as natural as breathing, but for others it can cause a simulation of a cat jumping out of its skin. Cultures tend to vary heavily in their social practices, but at least you can consider yourself warned if you are from a culture that doesn't practice much physicality in their social engagements. Don't be put-off, though - you're not likely to find the locals touching you up if they don't know you.

Finally, a word on English speaking: in cities, you should fare rather well, as is the case with most places in Europe. As is also usually the case, the level of English speakers drops the more into the countryside you travel. Even in cities, don't be alarmed if the passerby you stopped for directions doesn't understand you. In such cases, it would be best to ask at a hotel or restaurant, or approach someone under the age of 30 - the youth are embracing speaking English as a second language much more than the generations before them.

Love

The Portuguese are as passionate in love as they are in life. A relationship with a local will likely consist of many messages every day, lots of sweet nothings and and attitude that the world would end without you. It is all rather genuine, too, as most Portuguese folks, both men and women, are family oriented and traditionally minded. The local romance culture is centered around expression, openness and devotion. Perfect if you're into that sort of thing!

Despite being one of the more traditional and conservative places in Europe, Portugal legalised same-sex marriage in 2010. This may come as a relief to any readers who are of the related persuasion. The general attitude toward same-sex relationships has also softened with each passing generation, so whether you're a man or a woman dating a man or a woman, you have no need to fear missing out on Portugal's wonderful culture and its warm, vibrant people.

Useful Words

Below are some of the most common words (such as please and thank you) that can help you get by when visiting Portugal. Learning a few could also help you make a good impression on the locals! Phonetic pronunciation is in parentheses ().

  • Bom dia (bom dee-ah) - Good morning/general greeting.
  • Boa tarde (bo-ah tard-eh) - Good afternoon/general greeting. Used after noon.
  • Boa noite (bo-ah noy-e-teh) - Good evening/general greeting. Used after the sun goes down.
  • Muito obrigado (mu-ee-to ob-ree-gah-doh) - Thank you very much.
  • Obrigado (ob-ree-gah-doh) - Thanks. More informal than Muito Obrigado, but acceptable in shops etc.
  • Por favor (por fah-vorr) - Please.
  • De nada (deh nada) - You're welcome.
  • Adeus (ah-dee-oss) - Good bye. Usuable all day.
  • Sim (seem) - Yes.
  • Não (now) - No.
  • Desculpe (desh-cul-pah) - Sorry.
  • Perdão (per-dowl) - Excuse me. Useful when you want to pass by someone on the street.

Do's

  • Be sure to properly greet those you meet!
  • Act like people are watching
  • Make use of the common words above! English isn't widely spoken in Portugal outside of the youth

Don'ts

  • Be abashed by touching during conversation. The Portuguese are a warm and social people
  • Speak with your hands
  • Act boistrously or ungraciously
