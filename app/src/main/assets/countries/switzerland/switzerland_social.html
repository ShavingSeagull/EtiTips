A nation famed for its breathtaking landscape and impressive mountain ranges, Switzerland is a hiker's and artist's paradise in equal measure. The country is unusual in that it is a natural combination of multiple cultures and languages, having Germany, France, Austria and Italy as neighbours. Whether you come for the chocolate, the skiing or for business, you are certain to never forget this idyllic nation.

The capital city is Bern and the currency is the Swiss Franc (Fr. or CHF).

Behaviour

The Swiss often get lumped in with the Germans and this is perhaps no surprise - over 60% of Swiss people speak German (or the Swiss dialect of German), vastly more than any of the other main languages, French, Italian and Romansh. The general nature of this majority can often resemble the nature of the Germans, too, with some of the stereotypes proving true for both nationalities. Naturally, this isn't true for all Swiss and, certainly, the further south-west you go, the more French the people become in attitude.

To conduct oneself well in Switzerland, it is important to keep a level head as they do. You will get nowhere if you act boistrously or rudely. Shouting in the street should absolutely be avoided and keeping a good degree of social grace would be a wise idea. Be sure to speak politely (always say please and thank you), but directly - the Swiss aren't known for beating around the bush. The people here are big on proper greetings, so be sure to shake hands with everyone you are introduced to, if you find yourself in such a scenario. For friends, a handshake between men is appropriate and for two women or a man and a women, air kisses on the cheeks are expected - three of them, in fact, starting with the right. It is fairly common to be greeted by shop staff and you will be expected to return the gesture, so check out the common words section below for some handy things to make you stand out in the right way!

The Swiss aren't the most outgoing bunch, so don't expect idle chitchat with strangers on the street. It's also worth noting that the direct manner of the language here makes some typical topics for small-talk in English a bit redundant. For example, we often say "How are you?" when we meet someone in English and this is seen as largely rhetorical - reply with anything besides "Fine, thanks" and you'll probably draw eyerolls. However, in Switzerland such a question would be taken seriously and answered seriously, so do bear this in mind.

Finally, a note on the Swiss people's famed love of privacy: it's very true that people prefer to keep their cards close to their chest here. When you meet someone for the first time, regardless of the setting, don't ask probing questions! This is seen as highly rude and will cause the worst first impression you could hope for. In some cultures, such as in America, telling your life story to a stranger can be rather common, but you should absolutely refrain from that in Switzerland.

Love

The attitude to romance in Switzerland will vary as much as the culture does, honestly. Find yourself in the north, where the strong German influence is and you'll find the more German approach of no displays of affection and and a more...stoic...take on dating to be more prevalent. Go further south toward either the French or Italian border and you'll find a more liberal attitude in line with the neighbouring country. Seeing as Switzerland is a conservative minded nation, however, it would probably be wise to err on the side of caution when visiting with a loved one. Keep private matters to a private setting.

Despite the naturally conservative nature of the people, same-sex relationships are legal in Switzerland and same-sex marriage is soon to be passed into law, so please bear this in mind if your personal views differ from the local population's. As with the attitude toward public displays of affection, the Swiss aren't bothered what you do in your private life as long as you keep it to yourself. That being said, people in relationships of any nature should find little resistance from the native people, providing you conduct yourself properly.

Useful Words

Below are some of the most common words (such as please and thank you) that can help you get by when visiting Switzerland. Note, that there are four official languages in Switzerland, but Swiss-German is by far the most common, so that is what is listed below. Also note, Swiss-German is a spoken language, but not an official language itself. All written things in Switzerland are done so in standard German, so the words below would be different to write than they are to say! There is no spelling for Swiss-German because it isn't a written language, so what you see below is the closest written interpretation of the words spoken. If in doubt and if you know how to speak normal German, you can also use that - virtually all Swiss-German speakers also speak normal German.

Learning a few could also help you make a good impression on the locals! Phonetic pronunciation is in parentheses ().

  • Grüezi (gru-eh-tzee) - Hello. A popular greeting used by most, whether on the phone, in shops or hiking
  • Hoi (hoy) - Hi. More informal than Grüezi.
  • Merci vilmal (mer-cee vil-mahl) - Thank you very much.
  • Dangge (dahng-ger) - Thanks. Shorter and more informal than Merci Vilmal, but useful in shops etc.
  • Bitte (bitter) - Please.
  • Uf Widerluege (oof veeder-looger) - Good bye. Used at all times of the day.
  • Tschüss (shoos) - Bye. Much more informal than Uf Widerluege.
  • Ja (yah) - Yes. Note how the 'J' is pronounced like a 'Y'.
  • Nein (nine) - No. Said just like the English number 9.
  • Entschuldigung (ent-shool-dee-goong) - Sorry/excuse me. Useful for mistakes or when trying to pass someone on the street.

Do's

  • Return greetings you might receive from shop staff etc
  • Conduct yourself in a civilised manner
  • Shake hands if you meet someone new

Don'ts

  • Shout down the street
  • Be discourteous
  • Ask personal questions when unfamiliar with someone
