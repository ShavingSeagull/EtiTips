The home of democracy and modern civilisation as we know it, Greece is perhaps the largest contributor to Western society out of the plethora of cultures and countries that make the region up. A proud nation with a very proud history spanning many thousands of years, the Greeks are responsible for most major facets of society and concepts of statehood. The country remains an archaeologist's and tourist's dream alike.

The capital city is Athens and the currency is the Euro (€).

Behaviour

The Greeks are hugely social and welcoming. Whether visiting an area you know well or going for the first time, you'll likely be met with enthusiastic hospitality. Aside from their grand hospitable nature, the Greeks are also very laid back, so it would certainly be wise to follow suit; if you have any demons in the closet, leave them at home. Greetings are also very important - a firm hand shake with men, women and even children is expected. Friends will often hug and kiss on the cheeks.

Personal space and restraint in conversation are two major areas that may conflict with you, depending on where you are from. Don't be alarmed if somebody touches you during conversation, it is just the way the people are. As for conversation, definitely expect to be asked personal questions within moments of meeting someone for the first time. There are many cultures that perceive this as rude, but please keep in mind that in Greece this is, in fact, the opposite of rude - it is seen as a way to get to know somebody better, without needless formality. Questions like, "Are you married?" and "Do you have children?" will be the most commonly asked things, so just answer them as if you were talking to an old friend. You will certainly be treated as such in return.

An important note about body language! When it comes to speaking with one's hands, the Italians usually take the top spot, but the Greeks aren't far behind. If you don't usually speak with your hands, it may help if you indulge a little while here. However, the most important thing to remember is that the body language you're used to likely won't work in Greece and in some cases it can be highly rude! The 'OK' sign, for example, is rather universal in its meaning. However, in Greece it is considered very offensive indeed (akin to flipping someone off). So, if you wish to convey acknowledgement, use the trusty thumbs up, which is acceptable here. Also, nodding your head in agreement or shaking it for disagreement will likely be misunderstood. The Greeks tend to voice their agreement rather than show it, but when they do, they give a small downwards inclination of the head and that's it. For disagreement, they give a sharp backwards nod of the head. Definitely keep body language in mind when visiting Greece, as much of the world tends to follow the same unwritten rules and gestures.

Love

While generally laid back, the Greeks are a passionate bunch. Relationships and families remain rather traditional here, which is echoed in the dating scene. Fortunately, it seems the Greeks have a fondness for foreigners! A foreign woman in a bar is far more likely to be approached by a local man than a Greek woman would be. If you find yourself dating a Greek, expect to be showered with compliments about your appearance, your accent and perhaps your homeland. We weren't kidding when we said they were welcoming...

"Meeting the friends will make or break the relationship", or so the old saying goes. Nowhere is it more true than in Greece. If things are going well, after a few dates you may be brought to meet the Parea - the best friends. This may be a mix of men and women, lifelong friends and university friends etc, but know that gaining their approval is essential. If the Parea don't like you, you can probably start looking for that proverbial other fish in the sea.

Useful Words

Below are some of the most common words (such as please and thank you) that can help you get by when visiting Greece. Learning a few could also help you make a good impression on the locals! Phonetic pronunciation is in parentheses ().

  • Καλημέρα (ka-lee-mer-ah) - Good morning/general greeting.
  • Καλησπέρα (ka-lee-sper-ah) - Good afternoon/Good evening/general greeting. Used from late afternoon until the sun goes down.
  • Γειά σου (yah-soo) - Hello/Goodbye. Note it can be used as a greeting and a farewell.
  • γεια (yah) - Hi/Bye. More informal than Γειά σου, but acceptable in shops etc.
  • Ευχαριστώ (eff-ha-ree-sto) - Thank you.
  • Παρακαλώ (para-kah-low) - Please/You're welcome. Yup, it's the same word for both meanings.
  • Ναί (neh) - Yes.
  • όχι (oh-hee) - No.
  • Συγνώμη (see-gno-mee) - Sorry/Excuse me. Useful when you want pass by someone or if you wish to apologise.

Do's

  • Be sure to properly greet people
  • Speak with your hands if you are able to. It will certainly help you to be understood
  • Take a load off and kick back. The Greeks prefer to 'pass' time, not 'use' it

Don'ts

  • Use the 'OK' hand gesture! It is highly rude
  • Forget that the body language you likely use for 'yes' and 'no' won't work here
  • Be afraid to use the common words above. It may even get you a free shot of Ouzo at the local restaurant (not guaranteed)...
