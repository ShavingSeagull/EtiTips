Sadly, there seems to be two sides to the Mexican coin. For many foreigners, the word 'Mexico' produces images of violent drug cartels and a lawless society. While the cartels do exist (and honestly, where don't they?), they don't shape general Mexican life in the slightest. Leave the doom-and-gloom news stories behind because Mexico is a bright, vibrant country with a wonderful native population, great food, great drink and a welcoming culture.

The capital city is Mexico City and the currency is the Peso ($).

Behaviour

Greetings are an important social custom in Mexico. Whether meeting someone for the first time or the hundreth, you are expected to greet them when you meet and give a farewell when you part ways. Typically, this will be by using Buenos Dias/Buenas Tardes/Buenas Noches (good morning/ good afternoon/good evening), with a simple Adiós (goodbye) as a farewell. Note, too, that when greeting someone, a handshake or an air kiss is expected - a firm handshake for men (or a hug for friends) and an air kiss on both cheeks for two women. Kisses also apply to a man and a woman, but only if they're friends; if the lady is a new acquaintance, a short bow is expected instead.

The Mexican people are extremely laid back and easy going. Their attitude is definitely one to adopt when you're in the country, especially if you're looking to relax! Mexicans also tend to be very polite, hence the importance of greetings. Always remember to say please (Por Favor) and thank you (Gracias) when dealing with the locals and, even better, do so in Spanish. Seriously, the locals place a lot of appreciation in foreigners who try to use even a totally elementary degree of Spanish. Mexcians are foreigner-friendly, but they do make a differentiation between 'barbaric' uncultured foreigners and educated, cultured ones. Always try to come across as the latter.

The concept of 'personal space' doesn't exist much in Mexico. It is very commonplace and an accepted practice to include physicality during conversation - think pats on the shoulder or touches of the arm etc. For some, this is as natural as breathing, but for others it can cause a simulation of a cat jumping out of its skin. Cultures tend to vary heavily in their social practices, but at least you can consider yourself warned if you are from a culture that doesn't practice much physicality in their social engagements. Following this is that the physicality tends to be elongated - meaning a hand shake or a squeeze of the arm could last several seconds. Again, be prepared for this, but don't flinch as this is seen as rude.

Regarding conversation: most Mexicans will relish the chance to practice their English with you, so don't be surprised if you have a conversation struck-up with you every so often. Note, though, that the way in which the locals speak is different to what most people are used to in the western world. A lot of countries speak with a very direct manner, but this will be viewed as pushy in Mexico. Instead, match the laid back nature of the locals and don't be afraid to ask (or be shocked if asked) about somebody's family of their general health. This isn't nosey in the slightest and stems from the Mexican people's love for socialisation. Good topics for conversation with a local would be their family, their culture, their country or your own.

Finally, a word on English speaking: in cities, you should fare rather well, as is the case with most places in the western world. As is also usually the case, the level of English speakers drops the more into the countryside you travel. Even in cities, don't be alarmed if the passerby you stopped for directions doesn't understand you. In such cases, it would be best to ask at a hotel or restaurant, or approach someone under the age of 30 - the youth are embracing speaking English as a second language much more than the generations before them. As we said before, the importance of making even the slightest effort with speaking Spanish is virtually priceless here.

Love

Romance in Mexico is a mix of American influenced dating (in the big cities) and traditional courtship everywhere else. One thing to note, however, is that chivalry is still very much alive in Mexico and men are expected to perform the niceties for women that once existed everywhere. Whether that be opening a door, pulling out a chair or offering your jacket in cooler weather, a man will be expected to be a man and Mexican women will respond in kind, utilising their femininity to display their attractiveness. If you're someone of a younger generation reading this, just ask your grandparents how they dated when they were young and you'll learn everything you need to know to pursue romantic relations in Mexico.

If you do manage to hit it off, know that relationships in Mexico can function a little differently than other places. Both men and women like to be in contact with their significant other quite a lot and can come across as intense or 'clingy', depending on what you are used to. This stems from a romantic culture steeped in tradition and passion, rather than a concept of smothering or control. Families will also play a huge role in the relationship, so do be prepared for this!

Useful Words

Below are some of the most common words (such as please and thank you) that can help you get by when visiting Mexico. Learning a few could also help you make a good impression on the locals! Phonetic pronunciation is in parentheses ().

  • Buenos dias (bweh-noss dee-ass) - Good morning/general greeting.
  • Buenas tardes (bweh-nass tar-dess) - Good afternoon/general greeting. Used after noon.
  • Buenas noches (bweh-nass noh-chess) - Good evening/general greeting. Used after the sun goes down.
  • Muchas gracias (moo-chass grath-ee-ass) - Thank you very much. Note the 'c' is said as a 'th'.
  • Gracias (grath-ee-ass) - Thanks. More informal than Muchas Gracias, but acceptable in shops etc.
  • Por favor (por fah-vorr) - Please.
  • De nada (deh nada) - You're welcome.
  • Hola (oh-la) - Hello.
  • Adiós (ah-dee-oss) - Good bye. Usuable all day.
  • Si (see) - Yes.
  • No (no) - No. Yup, just like in English.
  • Lo siento (low see-en-toe) - Sorry.
  • Discúlpeme (dis-cuul-peh-meh) - Excuse me. Useful when you want to pass by someone on the street.
  • Habla inglés? (abla-in-gleys) - Do you speak English?. Notice you don't pronounce the 'H'. Always ask this rather than assuming they do

Do's

  • Be sure to properly greet those you meet!
  • Be easy going in Mexico. Uptightness isn't looked upon well
  • Make use of the common words above! English isn't widely spoken in Mexico outside of the youth and any effort will make you look cultured, which the locals love

Don'ts

  • Be abashed by touching during conversation. The Mexicans are a warm and social people
  • Speak too directly here
  • Be afraid to ask about somebody's family during conversation
