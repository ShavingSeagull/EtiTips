Spanish culture is dominated by food, drink, socialising and festivals. When one thinks of a rich cultural European nation, Spain is one of the first places to come to mind for most people, along with perhaps France or Italy and that's for good reason: with many a great sports star and a history that has spanned many hundreds of years, Spain has had an impact on the world like few others. Spanish is the second most natively spoken language in the world behind Chinese, with the vast majority of the Southern American continent speaking it as their first language.

The official language is Spanish (with official regional alternatives, such as Catalan) and the national religion is Christianity.

Religion

Spain is majority Christian, with a massive 71% of the population identifying with the Roman Catholic branch of the faith (as of 2019). The second highest affiliation is non-religious, coming in at only 25% of the populace, showing that, unlike much of the rest of western Europe, the younger generations are not moving away from religious affiliations, although many hold an identity without actively practicing. Though typically seen as a fairly religious people, the Spanish constitution values secularism, with a clear line between church and state.

Many Spanish, as with quite a few other things, can prove to be very passionate about their religion, so it would be wise to keep an err of decorum should you find yourself discussing the subject. It would be ill advised to chastise Christianity in front of Spaniards, as doing so will likely make you appear a 'pig-headed foreigner', so to speak. Discussing religion is a sensitive area in most places, but in countries like Spain, it's probably best just avoided altogether.

Dress

The Spanish favour a surprising degree of order to their dress sense. They generally side with dressing well whatever the occasion (so leave your sweatpants at home!), but certain things should be worn in certain situations. A great example of this is swimwear/beachwear, which should only be worn at the beach. In some cities like Barcelona, it is even illegal to wear swimwear around the city and could land you a fine of €100-€200. It may sound obvious to keep swimwear on the beach, but we tend to let go of our sensibilities a bit when we go on holiday, especially to very warm countries like Spain. Nevertheless, dress well, dress sensibly and dress for the occasion!

Heed this warning about dressing in churches: Spain has a beautiful variety of cathedrals and churches that are popular with tourists, but they are still places of worship and therefore a certain standard of dress is expected if you want to be let through the door. For the ladies, the shoulders (and cleavage, if any) should be covered (a light scarf will do) and for the gents, no bare chests and switch from shorts to any form of trouser if you can. Jeans, linen trousers and slacks are all fine. Sandals are acceptable for men and women, too.

Everyday Life

As with Italy just over the water, time keeping isn't a huge thing in Spain. The running joke for expats hiring a contractor is that they work on 'Spanish time'. Indeed, the pace of life is definitely a lot slower here, so it's best to put the idea of sticking to sharp schedules aside, whether you are moving to Spain or just here on holiday. Take your time and enjoy what the country has to offer - the locals certainly will.

Family is a hugely important concept in Spanish culture. Families here tend to be large and they tend to be close, so this is certainly something to bear in mind if you are dating/marrying a local, for example. The saying of 'marrying into the family' exists everywhere, but nowhere is it more applicable than in Spain. Boys will be close to their mothers and elders will be held in high esteem. Children are also massively respected in Spain, with most parents doting on their children constantly. This can be helpful in restaurants, for example, where a lot of waiters will make an effort to bring a smile to your children's faces, as this is the nature of the culture. As noted on the Food & Drink tab, bringing a small gift for any children is also customary when eating at a Spanish host's house, so do bear this in mind if invited over by friends.

Work Life

Spanish businesses operate according to a traditional hierarchical structure, though this is gradually changing in the modern age. If conducting business with Spaniards, however, do bear rank and title in mind as they likely will be. The Spanish work day can seem very long to foreigners, but with two hours often being taken for lunch (and siesta, depending on where you are), the time is made up later, which results in work days ending at 7-8pm. Owing to the cultural importance of the afternoon meal, it is also very unwise to schedule any meetings or appointments between noon and 4pm.

A foreigner in Spain for business? Then pay attention to the following tip as it may just save your deal: don't talk turkey at a lunch or dinner meeting! The Spanish like to conduct business with people they trust and meal times are for eating and socialising, not working. If you're invited to a meal-based meeting, spend the time getting to know your cohort personally rather than attempting to negotiate any business. If and when your associate wishes to talk business, they will likely do so at the office. Talking about sport (football/soccer in particular) or family life are two popular subjects here.

A quick note about time keeping: while the Spanish typically operate according to 'Spanish time', this most certainly does not apply to work life. If you have a meeting scheduled at 9am, you better make sure you turn up on time!

Do's

  • Dress well and dress for the right occasion
  • Indulge in the local culture! There is a plethora of things to experience
  • Be respectful toward children as they are hugely important in Spanish culture

Don'ts

  • Attempt to do business over meal times
  • Arrive on time, unless it's for work
  • Expect things done with any manner of speed. Kick back, relax and enjoy the fruits of this beautiful nation
