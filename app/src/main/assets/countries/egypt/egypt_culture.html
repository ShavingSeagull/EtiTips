The culture of Egypt is steeped in history and the evolution of human history. Few civilisations are as old or as well established as Egypt's, with thousands of years of contribution to society and development. In the modern day, Egypt prides itself on family values, hospitality and friendly social endeavours.

The official language is Arabic (though the national language is the Egyptian Arabic dialect) and the official religion is Islam.

Religion

Egypt is majority Muslim, with a massive 85% of the population identifying with the faith, the vast majority with the Sunni branch. The second highest affiliation is Coptic Christian, coming in at only 15% of the populace. Islam as a religion is also entwined in state affairs, as is the case with virtually all Muslim nations.

Islam has very strict rules that govern many aspects of daily life. This can range from the place of women in society, to what is acceptable to speak about when discussing the religion itself. There has been a lot of tension between Muslims and non-Muslims in the last couple of decades, so it is best, if you are a Western tourist, to be extra vigilant in the way you behave while in Muslim countries. Don't be alarmed, however - Egyptians are friendly and warm people who will be accommodating regardless of where you're from. That said, it would be extremely wise not to engage in discussion about religion, especially if you are non-religious. Under no circumstance should you talk about the Muslim prophet Mohammed; it is just a subject best avoided altogether. In many countries, speaking ill of him will cost you your life.

Dress

Egyptians dress conservatively, but like to dress well, as presentation denotes one's status and self worth. Bear in mind that Egypt is a country in northern Africa, so the average temperature is rather high. Linen trousers and stylish shirts/light jackets will be a good choice here. Ladies are not required to wear a headscarf, though do keep in mind that displaying too much flesh will be very frowned upon. Covering the shoulders, chest and legs is a very good idea.

If you intend on visiting religious sites while in Egypt, note that there will be a strict dress code. Both men and women will have to take their shoes off before entering and women will have to ensure that they cover their flesh even more than usual.

Everyday Life

Family is a hugely important concept in Egyptian culture. Families here tend to be large and they tend to be close, so this is certainly something to bear in mind if you are dating/marrying a local, for example. Children are important, as are elders, who are held in the highest esteem within Egyptian society and culture. If you spot an older person in need of help, you should definitely lend a hand as you will be setting an incredibly good impression with the locals.

While not a nation known for its outstanding football/soccer team (sorry, Egypt!), the passion for the sport runs high here. On match days, don't be surprised to see the streets littered with passionate fans. Indeed, if you're looking for an ice-breaker in conversation with a local man, just bring up football and, if you're aware of who he is, talk about Mohamed Salah's career. You will likely be stuck in conversation for some time!

Work Life

Egyptian businesses don't place huge value on time keeping, with many staff arriving a good thirty minutes after the official starting time. Office spaces also tend to be open, with employees happy to chat to one another. That being said, if you have business in Egypt, be sure you arrive on time! As is usually the case with time-lax countries, it is one rule for me and another rule for thee.

A foreigner in Egypt for business? Take note that business will not usually take place until your Egyptian counterpart decides they like you. The personal side of a working relationship is hugely important, so do take the time to get to know your cohort first. For many Westerners who are used to the opposite, the slower pace of proceedings may be a little frustrating, but do persevere as trying to conduct business in a Western fashion will prove futile. When it comes to negotiating, it is wise not to take a hard-sell approach, though do stay firm in your convictions as the Egyptians tend to be hard bargainers. Take note that voices may become raised during meetings. This is not a sign of aggression, it is merely the way Egyptians tend to speak, so please don't misconstrue such behaviour. Avoid scheduling meetings on a Thursday and Friday, as this tends to be the weekend in Egypt; Friday is the Muslim holy day, thus the Thursday usually completes the typical two days off. Also, avoid arranging anything during Ramadan, as businesses will be functioning on a reduced schedule.

Do's

  • Dress well and dress conservatively
  • Be sure to visit the historical sites! (Just remember to tip your tour guide)
  • Be respectful toward elders as they are hugely important in Egyptian culture

Don'ts

  • Attempt to do business on a Friday or during Ramadan
  • Show too much flesh, especially as a woman
  • Expect things done with any manner of urgency
