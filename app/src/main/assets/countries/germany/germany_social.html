With a history spanning over a thousand years, Germany is one of Europe's major nations and a huge global economy. Known for their industrious attitude and strong work ethic, the Germans have remained a wealthy nation inspite of recent global financial troubles over the last 10 years. Known during the Middle Ages as the Holy Roman Empire (despite not being holy, Roman or a true empire), the modern day Germany is no less powerful on the world stage.

The capital city is Berlin and the currency is the Euro (€).

Behaviour

Germans are famous for their stoic nature. They are often the butt of a joke about being humourless, despite having a healthy comic scene. It is true that most Germans tend to appear extremely methodical and level-headed, so don't mistake this for aloofness of rudeness if you speak to them. Germans have received a bad reputation for being arrogant when they go on holiday abroad and, while this is a matter for the individual to decide, their hospitality toward foreign visitors in Germany is without question.

To conduct oneself well in Germany, it is important to keep a level head as they do. You will get nowhere if you act boistrously or rudely. Shouting in the street should absolutely be avoided and keeping a good degree of social grace would be a wise idea. Be sure to speak politely, but directly - Germans aren't known for beating around the bush. Also, don't be put-off if someone you are speaking to doesn't smile much - it is just the German way and it certainly doesn't mean they don't like you! If you are greeting Germans in at least a partially formal setting, be sure to shake hands with everyone there. The nature of greetings and farewells also apply a little more in Germany than in other countries in Europe. It isn't uncommon to be greeted by shop staff, so check out the common words section below for some handy things to make you stand out in the right way!

Small talk isn't popular in Germany. If you find yourself talking to a stranger, the chances are you will be talking about religion, politics or philosophy - it isn't called the Land of Poets and Thinkers for nothing! This may actually come as a relief because many people struggle with small talk or find it superficial and pointless. You may have a more in-depth conversation with the average Joe here, but it's certain to be an eye-opening chat and could be a great way to start a a cultural exchange!

It is a long-running joke, especially in the UK, but you really shouldn't mention the war (World War II). Germans are not proud of their past in this respect and take every opportunity to move past it. Dragging up old issues, especially ones you likely weren't alive to experience, is a sure-fire way to make yourself deeply unpopular here. Best to leave the past to the past.

Love

You may think the stalwart nature of the Germans would make a romantic life difficult in Germany....and you'd be right, at least to a certain degree. Germans are notoriously anti-touchy-feely, so keep your hands to yourself! If you are travelling to Germany with your sweetheart, a similar behviour should be followed. Germans aren't fond of public displays of affection, so it's best to keep that stuff to the privacy of your hotel room.

Same sex relationships and marriages are legal in Germany, so please bear this in mind if your personal views differ from the local population's. Berlin was actually the gay capital of the world before Hitler took power (way back in the 1930s!) and the generally tolerant nature hasn't wavered much. As with the attitude toward public displays of affection, Germans aren't bothered what you do in your private life as long as you keep it to yourself. That being said, people in relationships of any nature should find little resistance from the native people.

Useful Words

Below are some of the most common words (such as please and thank you) that can help you get by when visiting Germany. Learning a few could also help you make a good impression on the locals! Phonetic pronunciation is in parentheses ().

  • Guten Morgen (gooten morgen) - Good morning/general greeting. Use until noon.
  • Guten Tag (gooten tahg) - Good day/general greeting. Use after noon until the sun goes down.
  • Guten Abend (gooten ahbend) - Good evening/general greeting. Use after the sun goes down.
  • Hallo (hallo) - Hello. Much more informal than any of the above, but useful in a pinch because of its similarity to the English 'hello'.
  • Danke schön (dahn-ker shern) - Thank you.
  • Danke (dahn-ker) - Thanks. More informal than danke schön.
  • Bitte (bitter) - Please.
  • Auf Wiedersehen (owf veeder-seyn) - Good bye. Used at all times of the day.
  • Auf Wiederhören (owf veeder-hern) - Good bye. Only used on the phone!
  • Tschüss (shoos) - Bye. More informal than Auf Wiedersehen.
  • Ja (yah) - Yes. Note how the 'J' is pronounced like a 'Y'.
  • Nein (nine) - No. Said just like the English number 9.
  • Entschuldigung (ent-shool-dee-goong) - Sorry/excuse me. Useful for mistakes or when trying to pass someone on the street.

Do's

  • Shake hands if you meet someone new
