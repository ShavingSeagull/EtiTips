Jamaica has a long history dating back to the colonial era and beyond. Gaining independence from the United Kingdom in only 1962, Jamaica has since moved on to become a tourist hotspot and a major influencer on western culture, with singers like Bob Marley introducing the popular Reggae style of music to the world and sports stars such as Usain Bolt changing the athletics scene forever.

The capital city is Kingston and the currency is the Jamaican Dollar ($).

Behaviour

Jamaicans are as social as they are traditional. Walking down a rural road and failing to greet a passerby will result in a very sour impression indeed. Likewise, a greeting should always be offered to shop staff when enquiring something of them or upon entering, if it's a small store. A firm handshake for two men will do the trick, while women often greet with a kiss on the cheek, starting with the right. As with a lot of other places, men will get more physical with each other, such as pats on the shoulder etc, when they're more familiarly acquainted or if they're related.

Titles are still widely used in Jamaica, especially with older people. You should always address someone as Mr, Mrs or Miss until invited to use their first name; don't do so before they say you can. Elders are held in high regard in Jamaican society, so using titles and maintaining respect toward older people is absolutely essential, as well.

Contrary to what you may be used to - depending on where you're from - Jamaicans tend to be rather direct. If they're thinking it, they'll probably say it. If you've got a big nose or no hair, don't be surprised if this becomes the butt of a joke. It may be easy to take offence to things like that, but it's best just laughed off and dealt with by engaging in some self-deprecating humour. Also, be sure to maintain eye contact when conversing with the locals. They view people with shifty eyes as suspicious, which is probably not an impression you want to set!

A note for women travelling alone: Expect a lot of...attention from the local men. Following the point above about directness, Jamaican men will make it very clear that they find you attractive. This can range from cheesy chat-up lines to full-blown pornographic suggestions. Some women come to Jamaica with the intention of finding an 'exotic lover', which has exacerbated the issue. Avoid eye contact, dress conservatively and be firm in your refusal, should you wish to avoid the propositions somewhat.

Love

As stated above, women will find no problem gaining the attention of the locals. Men will typically find it harder to impress the local ladies, though it isn't impossible. In either case, be sure to dress well and look presentable. For the men, taking charge and leading the date will make you attractive to Jamaican women, as they value traditional masculinity.

A warning for same-sex couples: Homosexuality is illegal in Jamaica and the population is rather religious. You don't need to avoid the country if you're gay, but you must ensure you don't publicise it. A lot of gay men own and/or work in the hotels in Jamaica, so asking for a double room won't be impossible, though under no circumstance should you engage in any form of public affection whatsoever. Even talking about homosexuality is a very risky idea here. Responses from the locals can range from disgust, arguments or ridicule, to threats and beatings.

Do's

  • Engage the locals in conversation, but don't forget their title if you know their surname!
  • Greet people you meet on the street in more rural areas
  • Maintain eye contact and speak directly

Don'ts

  • Be easily offended by humourous ribbing. Give as good as you get
  • Speak of or engage in homosexual acts of affection in public places. This is a matter of safety as well as etiquette
  • Leave your big girl pants at home if you're travelling to Jamaica as a solo woman. You will need to stand up for yourself against the very forward local men
